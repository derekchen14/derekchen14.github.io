## Motivation
Long-running agents drift silently over hours — small errors compound without any visible signal until it's too late. A billing surprise after a weekend agent run made the cost of poor observability visceral and immediate. Traditional request/response monitoring doesn't transfer to agents that loop, retry, and branch across extended sessions. Observability isn't optional for production AI agents; it's the difference between a trustworthy system and a black box.
## Latency Targets
Defining "acceptable" latency is harder when an agent can run for minutes, hours, or days. Each component — tool call roundtrips, model inference, queue wait, external API delays — can blow up a run on its own, so latency must be tracked per step, not end-to-end alone. Per-step SLOs catch slow steps before they cascade; without them, a single lagging tool call quietly stretches into a runaway runtime. Averages are misleading in long-running contexts, so prefer percentile tracking — p50, p95, and p99 — to surface tail latency issues early. In practice this means maintaining a p50 vs. p99 budget per turn, a clear end-to-end vs. per-tool-call breakdown, and a hard ceiling that triggers a kill switch when a step or the whole run exceeds acceptable bounds.
## Token Accounting
Tracking token consumption at each step — not just at session end — is the most reliable way to catch runaway loops early. That means distinguishing prompt, completion, and cached tokens on every model call, and tracking context-window utilization to anticipate when an agent is nearing its limit. Three metrics stand out: per-turn input tokens (context growth rate), cache-hit rate (latency and cost), and tool-result tokens — the dominant consumer and the first place to check when counts spike. Alerting on sudden per-step spikes gives you an early warning before the session becomes too expensive to continue.
## Cost Modeling
Translating token counts into dollar figures in real time keeps cost visible alongside performance, rather than letting it surface as a surprise on the monthly bill. That visibility matters: during one weekend incident, an agent entered a retry loop on a malformed tool response late on a Friday, and with no real-time cost signals the loop ran uninterrupted for roughly 40 hours. By Monday morning it had consumed more than twelve times its expected budget. The postmortem found the failure mode was well-understood — what was missing was a per-run cost budget and an automatic halt trigger. That gap became the forcing function for adding trajectory modeling to the observability stack: project a run's final cost from its first few turns, then halt it when the projection exceeds budget while there is still money to recover.

Attribute costs to specific tasks, users, or tenants from the start. Accurate chargeback and capacity planning both depend on it, and retrofitting attribution later is painful. With attribution in place, cost-per-completed-task becomes the headline metric: one number capturing efficiency and financial health together. Tracking it over time shaped our sampling decisions directly. Evaluation runs had initially sampled uniformly across task types, but cost-per-completed-task varied sharply by category: retrieval-heavy tasks cost three to four times as much per completion as pure-reasoning tasks, yet the two were sampled at identical rates. Switching to cost-weighted sampling — more budget for cheap tasks, reserved slots for high-impact model improvements — cut eval spend by roughly 30 percent without reducing coverage of high-stakes categories. The metric also exposed a subtler signal: when cost-per-completed-task rose without a matching drop in success rate, the model had started taking longer tool-call paths to the same answers, pointing directly at prompt or retrieval regressions.
## Dashboards
A live per-agent timeline — showing step count, cumulative tokens, elapsed time, and current cost — gives operators an at-a-glance picture of any running agent. Beneath that summary layer, a step-level drill-down view lets engineers inspect individual tool calls or model invocations when something looks off. Fleet-level aggregates round out the picture by enabling comparisons across runs, users, and task types over time. The key design insight is that dashboards serve two distinct audiences: operators who need summary health signals to know whether everything is within normal bounds, and debuggers who need raw traces to understand exactly what happened inside a specific run. Building explicitly for both avoids the common failure mode of a dashboard that is technically rich but practically useless for the person on call. Latency-percentile views — surfacing p50, p95, and p99 side by side — are an essential panel for spotting tail-latency regressions that averages would otherwise hide. Per-tool token panels complement this by breaking cumulative token counts down to the individual tool level, making it immediately obvious which call is responsible when token consumption spikes unexpectedly.
## Takeaways
Good observability surfaces problems before they become costly surprises — not after. The right instrumentation layer is built step-by-step rather than bolted on following a production incident, and the time to start is before the first real workload hits.

Token accounting and cost modeling together provide both the technical and financial view of agent health: one tells you what the agent is doing, the other tells you what it's costing. The practical path forward is to start with latency targets and a simple dashboard, then add cost modeling and alerting incrementally as your understanding of the system deepens.
